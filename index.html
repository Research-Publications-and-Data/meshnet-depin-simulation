Digital money & tokenization · from policy to execution

Research for real-world policy and operating constraints.

Stablecoins, tokenized deposits, and cash-management products are building a parallel dollar infrastructure. The same token produces different regulatory, monetary, and stress outcomes depending on who routes it. This research measures those differences at the gateway layer: the issuers, exchanges, and protocols that determine what the dollar actually does in digital markets.

Signature frameworks

Four frameworks that classify, measure, and stress-test the infrastructure behind digital-dollar products.

Track A Digital money, tokenization, and market infrastructure

CLII (Control Layer Intensity Index)

Scores regulatory intensity at the gateway layer across five dimensions. The CLII measures who is regulated, not who is fragile: three Tier 1 gateways with near-identical scores produced opposite outcomes during SVB based on entity-specific banking exposure, not token properties.

View CLII framework

MVEP (Minimum Viable Equivalence Pack)

Nine categories testing whether a tokenized product maintains legal, operational, and economic equivalence to the traditional instrument under stress. Each category specifies the required artifact and the pass/fail test. Reverse-engineered from the six failure modes Circle's SVB weekend exposed.

View MVEP framework

Credit migration model

Tracks what happens to bank deposits when they migrate into stablecoin reserves: which balance sheets grow, which shrink, and how credit creation and funding costs change as tokenized products absorb deposit volume.

View credit migration model

Regime dashboard

Ten signals classifying who currently controls value capture and operational liability: the issuer or the control layer. Reads routing defaults, sweep behavior, hold mechanics, and fee structures as they change. A shift in classification changes who the regulator can reach and who captures the economics.

Request regime dashboard
Track B Tokenomics and physical network systems (DePIN)

Who earns the token matters more than who holds it. Four papers measuring how emission schedules, slashing penalties, and governance structures determine whether a token economy sustains productive contribution or concentrates into extraction. Parameters calibrated to Helium, Filecoin, and Geodnet; 240-run Monte Carlo ensembles stress-tested across bull, bear, competitor-entry, and regulatory-shock conditions.
