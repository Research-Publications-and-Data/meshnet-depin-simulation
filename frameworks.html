Three dollar objects, one wallet

Payment stablecoins, tokenized deposits, and tokenized yield products look identical in a wallet but diverge by reserve structure, redemption mechanics, and stress behavior.

Payment stablecoins

Reserve-backed tokens designed for par redemption. Narrow reserve requirements, no yield to the holder, money-like supervisory treatment under GENIUS. The operating constraint: reserves must be liquid enough to honor redemptions during a stress run without selling assets at a loss.

Tokenized deposits

Bank liabilities in token form. They inherit deposit insurance, bank supervision, and the credit-creation mechanics of bank balance sheets. They are a fundamentally different object from reserve-backed stablecoins because the issuing bank lends against the deposits, creating credit that stablecoin reserves do not.

Yield wrappers and sweeps

Tokenized money-market fund shares, Treasury wrappers, and automated sweep programs. These are investment products, not payment instruments. A stablecoin that cannot pay yield creates a vacuum that adjacent products fill: whoever controls the default sweep (the wallet, the exchange, the platform) captures the yield margin.

Same wallet UX. Different rights, backstops, and failure sequences.

Core frameworks

The work does not stop at commentary. It produces tools teams can use to underwrite, govern, and escalate real operating decisions.

CLII (Control Layer Intensity Index)

A five-dimension heuristic scoring regulatory intensity at the gateway layer. Regulatory License 25%, Reserve Transparency 20%, Freeze/Blacklist Capability 20%, Compliance Infrastructure 20%, Geographic Restrictions/Sanctions 15%. The CLII measures who is regulated, not who is fragile: three Tier 1 gateways with near-identical scores produced opposite outcomes during SVB based on entity-specific banking exposure, not token properties.

Related paper: Routing the Dollar

MVEP (Minimum Viable Equivalence Pack)

A nine-gate diligence pack for testing whether the tokenized version really works like the traditional asset under stress, including rights parity, recordkeeping, auditability, and settlement finality.

Credit migration model

Tracks what happens to bank deposits when they migrate into stablecoin reserves: which balance sheets grow, which shrink, and how credit creation and funding costs change as tokenized products absorb deposit volume.

Related paper: Dollar v3 / The Control Layer War

Regime dashboard

Ten signals classifying who currently controls value capture and operational liability: the issuer or the control layer. Reads routing defaults, sweep behavior, hold mechanics, and fee structures as they change. A shift in classification changes who the regulator can reach and who captures the economics.

Current agenda

The recurring question is not whether dollars tokenize. It is where yield, liability, control, and crisis-time reliability end up once they do.

Application layer obligations

How web-hosted interfaces, routing defaults, and public-facing transaction layers become the real policy surface for screening, disclosures, and compliance controls.

Yield placement and wrappers

How yield moves up-stack into wrappers and sweeps when payment objects are constrained to stay money-like, and which firms end up controlling the default cash-management path.

Deposit mobility and credit migration

How tokenization changes bank funding, deposit stickiness, and the path credit takes when balances migrate into stablecoins, deposit tokens, or wrapper products.

Holds, finality, and evidence

What must exist for institutional tokenization to survive scrutiny: hold workflows, notice and appeal logic, reconciliation, audit trails, finality definitions, and reorg handling.

How the program is run

Questions ranked by the cost of being wrong

The work starts with the questions where being wrong would do the most damage to a balance sheet, a policy decision, or a live operating workflow.

Evidence tied to named sources

Claims only stay if they can be tied to a versioned source, a reproducible dataset, or a repeatable operating check.

Metrics chosen because they change decisions

Thresholds exist to move an owner, escalate a risk, trigger a redesign, or stop the work. If a metric changes nothing, it gets cut.

Publication gated on readiness

Work is published when the evidence supports real operating decisions and the launch standard is clearly met.
